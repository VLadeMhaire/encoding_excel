Individual Income Tax (TRAIN Law) Effective Jan 1, 2023

Under the Tax Reform for Acceleration and Inclusion (TRAIN Law, RA 10963), the Philippine income tax system uses a graduated schedule. The current Tax Schedule 2, effective January 1, 2023 onwards, features reduced rates compared to the 2018–2022 schedule.

Key TRAIN Provisions
  • Annual taxable income up to ₱250,000 is fully exempt from income tax
  • Minimum wage earners (MWEs) are exempt from income tax on statutory minimum wage and holiday/OT/hazard pay
  • First ₱90,000 of 13th month pay and other benefits is tax-free
  • Mandatory contributions (SSS, PhilHealth, Pag-IBIG) are non-taxable and deductible from gross income
  • No basic personal or additional exemptions; these are integrated into the ₱250,000 threshold

Self-Employed & Professionals (SEPs) — Optional 8% Flat Tax
  • SEPs with annual gross sales/receipts not exceeding ₱3,000,000 (VAT threshold) may opt for an 8% flat tax on gross sales/receipts and other non-operating income in excess of ₱250,000
  • The 8% option is in lieu of the graduated income tax and the 3% percentage tax
  • Must be elected at the start of the taxable year on the first BIR Form 1701Q filing
  • Not available to partners in a general professional partnership or those with mixed-income (compensation + business)
Substituted Filing Employees receiving purely compensation income from a single employer whose income tax has been correctly withheld are not required to file an annual ITR — the employer's BIR Form 2316 (stamped "received" by BIR) serves as a substitute.

Mandatory Contributions — 2025 Rates Updated Jan 2025

Employees and employers must remit mandatory contributions to three government agencies. All contributions are non-taxable and deducted from gross income for income tax computation.

Social Security System (SSS) — SSS Circular 2024-06, effective Jan 1, 2025
  • Total rate: 15% of Monthly Salary Credit (MSC) — increased from 14% (2023–2024)
  • Employer share: 9.5% + ₱10/₱30 Employee Compensation (EC) contribution
  • Employee share: 5% of MSC — increased from 4.5%
  • MSC range: Minimum ₱5,000 / Maximum ₱35,000 per month
  • Max employee contribution: ₱1,750/month · Max employer contribution: ₱3,530/month (incl. EC)
  • Law: Republic Act No. 11199 (Social Security Act of 2018)
Philippine Health Insurance Corporation (PhilHealth) — Effective 2025
  • Premium rate: 5% of Monthly Basic Salary (MBS)
  • Split: 2.5% employer + 2.5% employee (equal sharing)
  • Salary floor: ₱10,000/month · Salary ceiling: ₱100,000/month
  • Min contribution: ₱500/month total (₱250 each) · Max contribution: ₱5,000/month total (₱2,500 each)
  • Law: Republic Act No. 11223 (Universal Health Care Act)
Home Development Mutual Fund (Pag-IBIG / HDMF) — HDMF Circular 460, effective Feb 2024
  • Employee share: 2% of monthly compensation
  • Employer share: 2% of monthly compensation
  • Maximum Fund Salary (MFS): ₱10,000/month — increased from ₱5,000
  • Maximum contribution: ₱200 employee + ₱200 employer = ₱400/month total
  • Employees earning above ₱1,500/month must contribute the maximum ₱400 total

Understanding Value-Added Tax (VAT)

Value-Added Tax (VAT) is a consumption tax imposed at every stage of the supply chain on the sale, barter, exchange, lease, or importation of goods, properties, and services in the Philippines. The standard rate is 12% (effective since February 1, 2006).

Key Characteristics of VAT
  • Indirect Tax: VAT is ultimately borne by the final consumer; businesses act as tax collectors
  • Multi-Stage Tax: Collected at each stage of production and distribution
  • Credit-Invoice Method: Input VAT on purchases can be credited against Output VAT on sales
  • Registration Threshold: Mandatory for persons whose gross sales/receipts exceed ₱3,000,000 in any 12-month period

How VAT is Computed:

  1. Business charges 12% VAT on sales → Output VAT
  2. Business pays VAT on its purchases → Input VAT
  3. Remit the difference (Output VAT − Input VAT) to BIR quarterly
  4. If Input VAT > Output VAT, the excess becomes a VAT credit carry-over (or refund, if qualified)
2023 Change: Monthly VAT Filing Abolished (RMC No. 5-2023) Effective January 1, 2023, the mandatory monthly VAT Declaration (BIR Form 2550M) was abolished. VAT-registered taxpayers now only need to file the Quarterly VAT Return (BIR Form 2550Q) within 25 days after each taxable quarter. Optional monthly filing via Form 2550M is still allowed under RMC 52-2023, but quarterly filing remains mandatory.

Important Notes on VAT Registration
  • Once VAT-registered, a taxpayer must remain registered for at least 3 years before cancellation
  • Voluntary VAT registration is available to businesses below the ₱3,000,000 threshold
  • Change in registration status requires filing BIR Form 1905
  • Export-oriented enterprises registered in PEZA/BOI ecozones may be subject to 0% VAT on local purchases
  • RR 10-2025 clarified zero-rating rules for export enterprises effective April 1, 2025

CREATE Law (RA 11534) — Corporate Tax Incentives
  • Domestic corporations: 25% regular CIT (reduced from 30%)
  • Small domestic corporations (net taxable income ≤ ₱5M and total assets ≤ ₱100M): 20% CIT
  • Minimum Corporate Income Tax (MCIT): 2% of gross income (reduced to 1% from July 2020 to June 2023)
  • Registered Business Enterprises (RBEs) in ecozones may enjoy 5% special rate or enhanced deductions under CREATE MORE (RA 12066)

Deadline Extension Rules
  • If the deadline falls on a Saturday, Sunday, or legal holiday, the next working day becomes the deadline
  • eFPS filers are grouped into Groups A–E with staggered deadlines (5 working days before the standard deadline)
  • Manual filers must follow AAB (authorized agent bank) cut-off times — typically 5:00 PM
  • BIR may grant deadline extensions through Revenue Memorandum Circulars (RMCs) for natural disasters or system outages

Zero-Rated (0%) VAT Transactions
  • Export sales of goods to foreign customers (paid in foreign currency)
  • Foreign currency-denominated sales to non-resident buyers
  • Services rendered to persons doing business outside the Philippines
  • Sale of goods/services to entities registered in PEZA/BOI special economic zones (subject to RR 10-2025 rules effective April 1, 2025)
  • Processing or repackaging goods for export
  • Services rendered to international shipping/air transport operations
Note on VAT-Exempt Real Property Threshold: Per BIR RR 1-2024 and RMC 31-2025, the threshold for VAT-exempt sale of house and lot was increased to ₱3,600,000 effective January 1, 2024. This threshold is indexed to the Consumer Price Index every 3 years (next reset: January 1, 2027). Residential lots are not automatically exempt — check current BIR issuances.

Input Tax Credit Rules

VAT-registered taxpayers may credit input VAT on their purchases against output VAT on their sales, subject to the following rules:

Requirements for a Valid Input VAT Claim
  • VAT Invoice / Official Receipt: Must be issued by a VAT-registered supplier bearing the supplier's TIN and the word "VAT"
  • Business Purpose: Purchases must be directly attributable to a VATable business activity
  • Substantiation: Complete details on invoice (name, address, TIN, amount, description of goods/services)
  • Prescriptive Period: Input tax credits must be claimed within 2 years after the close of the taxable quarter when the sales were made (refund claims under Sec. 112)

Regular Purchases

100% input VAT creditable in the month/quarter of purchase

Supported by valid VAT invoice from a VAT-registered supplier

For goods for resale, use in business, or services consumed in taxable activity

Capital Goods ≥ ₱1,000,000

Input VAT amortized over 60 months (or the remaining useful life, if shorter)

Acquisition cost ₱1,000,000 and above per item

Unused amortized credit is forfeited upon sale/disposal of the asset

Transitional Input Tax

Available upon first registration or change from non-VAT to VAT

2% of beginning inventory value (or actual VAT on purchases, whichever is higher)

Applies to goods, materials, and supplies on hand at time of registration

Non-Creditable Input VAT

Purchases from non-VAT registered suppliers

Entertainment, amusement & recreation expenses (subject to limit)

Personal, family, and household expenses

Purchases for VAT-exempt activities (must be allocated)

Apportionment Rule — Mixed Transactions If a taxpayer engages in both taxable and exempt/zero-rated transactions, input VAT must be apportioned. Input VAT directly attributable to taxable sales is fully creditable. Input VAT on common costs is apportioned based on the ratio of taxable sales to total sales.
VAT Refund / Tax Credit Certificate (TCC)
  • A taxpayer with excess input VAT may apply for a refund or TCC under Section 112 of the NIRC
  • Application must be filed within 2 years after the close of the taxable quarter when the sales were made
  • The BIR must act on the claim within 90 days from submission of complete documents
  • Exporters and zero-rated taxpayers are common claimants; denial may be elevated to the Court of Tax Appeals (CTA)

Penalties for Non-Compliance Sec. 247–255 NIRC

Late Filing / Late Payment

25% Surcharge of tax due (Sec. 248A)

12% Interest per annum (double the legal interest rate under BSP Circular) on the unpaid amount

Compromise Penalty: ₱1,000 minimum (schedule under RMO 19-2007)

Willful Neglect / Fraud

50% Surcharge if deficiency is due to fraud, willful neglect, or failure to file (Sec. 248B)

Also applies if underdeclaration of income exceeds 30% of correct amount

Possible criminal prosecution (Sec. 255)

Failure to Register

Fine: ₱5,000 – ₱20,000

Imprisonment: 6 months – 2 years

Business closure order possible; civil and criminal liability

Failure to Issue Invoice / Receipt

Fine: ₱1,000 – ₱50,000 per violation (Sec. 264)

Revocation of Authority to Print (ATP) receipts

Temporary or permanent business closure

Underdeclaration of Income

50% surcharge if deficiency attributable to fraud

Tax evasion cases — fine of ₱30,000–₱100,000 and/or 2–4 years imprisonment (Sec. 254)

Civil liability for full tax + surcharges + interest

Failure to Keep Books

Fine: ₱1,000 – ₱25,000

Estimated assessment by BIR based on best available information

Records must be kept for 10 years for VAT-registered businesses

Voluntary Assessment and Abatement Program (VAAP)
  • The BIR periodically issues programs allowing taxpayers to settle deficiencies with reduced or waived surcharges
  • Generally applies to errors in previously filed returns discovered before formal audit/investigation
  • Basic tax + interest (no surcharge) if availed before issuance of Letter of Authority
  • Check the latest BIR Revenue Regulations for current amnesty/abatement programs
BIR Audit / Letter of Authority (LA)
  • The BIR issues a Letter of Authority authorizing examination of a taxpayer's books for a specific taxable year
  • Assessment must be issued within 3 years from the filing date (or due date, if later)
  • 10-year prescriptive period applies in cases of fraud, failure to file, or false return
  • Taxpayers have the right to protest a Preliminary Assessment Notice (PAN) within 15 days and a Final Assessment Notice (FAN) within 30 days
